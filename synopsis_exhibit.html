Treatment and Importance


Indian Independence from British rule on 15 August 1947 spearheaded decolonization in the post WW2 world. Indian Independence movement was a complex adaptive system as narrated in the historical background .

This exhibit is developed in accordance with subclass 2C Historical, Social and Special Studies category of FIP guidelines to narrate the revolutionary armed movement of Indian Freedom Struggle using philatelic materials and ephemera (Fig. 1) spanning Japanese-Occupation, Indian, British, German and Burmese philately (Fig. 2). While majority focus has been given to ca. 1940 onwards (hence Endgame), this movement is a culmination of decades-long endeavors by several revolutionary thinkers drawing inspiration from movements in other parts of the world. Understandably, significant part of the movement had been planned and executed beyond British ruled Indian subcontinent. The central idea of this story is defection of British-Indian subjects to fight for India’s independence. This idea was derived from the revolt of 1857, tried and tested during WW1, and finally succeeded in spirit and consequences during WW2 with the support of Axis powers.

Geopolitically, the revolutionary movement had tremendous implications. It disrupted British rule not only in India but in other parts of the empire through breaking down of peace treaties and inciting unrests during WW1. In the WW2 era, it shook British supremacy in the Indian subcontinent as the Japanese-supported military struggle under Subhas Chandra Boseʼs leadership not only recklessly precipitated Indian independence, but also made the British aware of the power of collective dissent as they were engaged in re-occupation efforts of Singapore and protecting other British interests in SE Asia. This exhibit is unique in comparison to other exhibits on Indian Freedom movement that underscore the Gandhian non-violent narrative. This is the ONLY comprehensive philatelic treatise on the non-Gandhian Indian freedom movement, developed through extensive historical and philatelic research. Frame 1 chronicles earlier events upto WW1 as Prologue and subsequent frames are arranged chronologically highlighting important events in Germany and South East Asia. Page 8 attempts to bridge a gap of nearly 25 years during which key revolutionary personalities were in exile and Gandhian non-violent movement dominated the narrative.

Philatelic Importance

Most of 19th century materials used in prologue, highlight key aspects of postal systems during military campaigns. During WW1, various interesting usages highlight key events associated with the revolutionary movement. During WW2, items associated with both Japanese Occupation and Allied defense have been displayed. Repetitions have been strictly avoided and contemporary unused stamps issued for either political or propaganda purposes have been included. The following (but not limited to) aspects of postal history have been displayed:
  • Rare camp postmarks and important postal history of the revolt of 1857.
  • FPOs of territorial campaigns in the Northeastern part of the Indian subcontinent.
  • Pertinent and rare usages of specific campaigns during World War 1.
  • British Censorships and evasion of such.
  • Lines of communications and mail disruptions during WW2.
  • Pertinent postal history of Japanese Occupation of Malaya and Burma.
  • Postal system of Indian Independence Leagues in Malaya under Japanese Occupation.
  • Postal innovations of British India during the NE frontier campaigns including RAF.
  • Postal Services during reclamation of territories.
  • Selective (2%) air-dropped propaganda materials (accepted in Aerophilately.
  • Victory celebrations for propaganda purposes. (1%)

Philatelic, Historical and Social Knowledge, Personal study, and Research

Historical and social events narrated here, are a result of considerable research translated into a philatelic exhibit. There is not a single treatise that exclusively narrates the topic of this exhibit. Most of the events can be found as sporadic events and piecing together a comprehensive continuous narrative in the form of an exhibit is the novelty. References have been made to research articles and recent publications on the historical aspects of the subject.

As can be noted, several items have been discovered by the exhibitor. Discovery has been claimed after thorough survey of several important and acclaimed collections made in the past. Prominent exhibits, collections and works of Masayoshi Tsuchiya, Derek Filby , David Tett, Gerald Sattin, Gerald Davis, and Dr. Soli Contractor have been carefully studied and referenced. Several important sales of Japanese Occupation collections and mixed lots in auctions have been thoroughly inspected (if possible). Additionally, archives of International Society of Japanese Philately, Indian Study Circle for Philately and Burma Study Circle have been carefully researched. Every item has been carefully described with the correct rate, route and the cancellation, often citing any associated articles. Articles have been published and/or communicated in prestigious journals on selected items.

Condition and Rarity

This exhibit consists of world class rarities of Japanese Occupation and Indian Philately. Commercially used mails during Japanese Occupation are scarce as it spanned only 4 years and postal regulations of one occupied territory was not necessarily translated into another exacerbated by civil and military censorships owing to spread of propaganda and misinformation during the war. During the very short period (1941-45) postal administrations were severely disrupted and multiple censorships were put in place. Almost all mails were within the occupied territories as services to and from adjacent British, French and Dutch colonies were stopped. For members of the Indian Independence Leagues (IIL), mails to families in India have not been recorded owing to censorship (both Japanese and British). Interception of telegraphic messaging were introduced and the rate was prohibitively expensive. This exhibit provides the only recorded evidence of telegraphic messaging system by IIL. Additionally, correctly addressing letters, following constantly changing censorship regulations was a difficult task for civilians. The exhibitor has recorded 12 different handstamps used in IIL correspondences (required for censorship purposes) and all of them have been displayed in this exhibit. Only one of these have been recorded before in a collection and the rest have been discovered by the exhibitor Correspondences from various camps of the INA are even scarcer. The exhibitor has recorded only 12 camp correspondences, 10 of which have been displayed here, 7 of which belonged to a specialized collection earlier, and the 3 have been discovered by the exhibitor. Since post re-occupation of Singapore most of INA and IIL documents and correspondences were destroyed (as per CIA report R&A 3142 S, June 8, 1945), any surviving mails would be quite scarce and no proper census has been attempted before. It is unlikely that the materials shown in this exhibit can be replicated with ease, as the previous attempt over a period of 40 years yielded only 7 camp mails and this is the ONLY comprehensive postal history treatise on this important aspect of Indian freedom movement abroad and the associated postal history.

Mails from British FPOs during WW2 are relatively less scarce, but the choice and discovery of mails on specific days of key events, especially in the height of disruption of services, makes the task of selecting materials infinitely difficult. Eg., mails around 30-31st of March, 1944 during the Imphal campaigns would be extremely rare since the lines of communication had been destroyed , and telegraphic communications were being set up. Such items have been selectively included in this exhibit.
